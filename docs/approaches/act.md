# Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

A "third-wave" contextual behavioral therapy with a large self-help/bibliotherapy
tradition, which makes much of it text-friendly.

## Model of change

Suffering is amplified by **experiential avoidance** (struggling to get rid of
unwanted inner experience) and **cognitive fusion** (being entangled with
thoughts as literal truth). The goal is **psychological flexibility** — contacting
the present moment and acting on values *even in the presence of* unwanted
thoughts and feelings, rather than eliminating them. Six "hexaflex" processes:
acceptance, defusion, present-moment contact, self-as-context, values, and
committed action.

## Signature techniques

- **Cognitive defusion** ("I'm having the thought that…," word-repetition).
- **Acceptance / willingness** exercises.
- **Values clarification** and **committed-action** goal-setting.
- Brief mindfulness / present-moment exercises.
- **Metaphors** (passengers on the bus, quicksand); the self-as-context observer
  stance.

## Best-evidenced indications

- A-Tjak et al. (2015), meta-analysis of 39 RCTs (n≈1,821): ACT **superior to
  control conditions (g ≈ 0.57)** across anxiety, depression, addiction, and
  somatic problems, with **no significant difference vs established treatments
  like CBT.**
- **APA Division 12:** strong research support for **chronic pain**; modest
  support for depression, OCD, mixed anxiety, and psychosis.
- **[CONTESTED]:** outcome equivalence with CBT is well-replicated; the claim of
  a *distinct mechanism* (psychological flexibility) is debated.

## Contraindications / poor fit

- Its abstract/metaphorical style suits some and confuses others.
- Acceptance framing can be **misheard as "give up"** by someone in crisis — not
  a first-line standalone for acute risk.

## Text-agent adaptation

- **Safe in chat:** defusion exercises, values-clarification worksheets,
  committed-action goal-setting, brief mindfulness scripts, ACT metaphors —
  highly self-help-friendly.
- **Not in chat / caution:** experiential exercises that surface intense affect;
  and never use acceptance language with someone in acute distress in a way that
  could read as endorsing hopelessness — the [crisis pivot](../../CONTEXT.md) and
  [person-centered](person-centered.md) validation come first.

## When Claudia reaches for this

When someone is stuck *fighting* their own thoughts and feelings — rumination,
self-criticism they can't argue their way out of, or a values–behavior gap.
Defusion and values work offer a different move than CBT's "test the thought":
loosen the grip rather than win the argument. Pairs well with
[mindfulness](mindfulness-self-compassion.md).
