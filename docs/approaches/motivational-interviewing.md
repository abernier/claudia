# Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Best understood as a **conversational style** for ambivalence, not a comprehensive
treatment — and an excellent fit for a text companion in that role.

## Model of change

Developed by Miller & Rollnick for ambivalence about change (originally
addictions). Motivation is **elicited from the person, not imposed**. Ambivalence
is normal; arguing for change (the "righting reflex") triggers resistance. The job
is to evoke and strengthen the person's own **change talk** within an accepting,
collaborative "spirit." Change follows when the person voices their own reasons
for it.

## Signature techniques

- **OARS** — Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries.
- Eliciting and reinforcing **change talk**; rolling with resistance.
- **Developing discrepancy** between values and current behavior.
- **Importance / confidence rulers** (0–10 scaling of readiness).
- The four processes: engaging, focusing, evoking, planning.

## Best-evidenced indications

- Best-established for **substance use and health-behavior change**, and as a
  **pre-treatment engagement booster**. Lundahl et al. (2010), 119 studies: **small
  but durable effects vs weak/no-treatment controls (g ≈ 0.28)** and **no
  significant advantage over other active treatments (g ≈ 0.09)** — efficient
  (fewer sessions) and good for engagement more than a large-effect standalone.
- **[CONTESTED]:** effect sizes are modest and heterogeneous; **fidelity strongly
  moderates outcome.**

## Contraindications / poor fit

- Not a comprehensive treatment for an established disorder on its own — best as a
  style/pre-treatment that then hands off to an active treatment.
- People already highly motivated may find it slow.

## Text-agent adaptation

- **Safe in chat:** the MI **spirit** (collaborative, non-judgmental,
  autonomy-supporting) plus OARS, importance/confidence rulers, and eliciting
  change talk are structured, low-risk, and well-suited to text. Great for helping
  someone set goals and explore ambivalence about a habit.
- **Not in chat / refer:** MI **for substance dependence** routes to specialist
  care — MI is not a substitute for medical management of withdrawal (see
  [refer-only.md](refer-only.md)).

## When Claudia reaches for this

When a person is **ambivalent** — "part of me wants to, part of me doesn't" — about
a habit, a decision, or engaging with the tools themselves. MI is also the
engagement layer that makes the other approaches land, so its spirit often runs
alongside the [person-centered](person-centered.md) base even when it isn't the
named approach.
